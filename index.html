Five signs of satiety

A baby can't say "I'm full." But the body shows it clearly:

  1. Releases the breast or bottle on their own. Doesn't reach back ‚Äî that means enough.
  2. Relaxes fists. A hungry baby clenches hands; a full one opens them.
  3. Falls asleep at the breast. If they were actively sucking for 10‚Äì15 minutes ‚Äî they're full. If they fell asleep after 2 minutes ‚Äî likely just tired.
  4. Turns head away. A reliable signal: don't insist.
  5. 6‚Äì8 wet diapers per day. The key objective metric: enough urine means enough milk.
If there are fewer than 4 wet diapers in 24 hours ‚Äî contact your pediatrician. This may indicate insufficient nutrition.

Breastfeeding vs formula: schedule differences

Breastfeeding. On demand, not by the clock. A newborn may ask for the breast every 1‚Äì1.5 hours ‚Äî this is normal and stimulates milk production. By 3‚Äì4 months, intervals increase to 2‚Äì3 hours.

Formula feeding. Interval of 2.5‚Äì3.5 hours. Formula digests slower than breast milk, so the baby stays full longer. Overfeeding shows as frequent spitting up and bloating.

Mixed feeding. Breast first (10‚Äì15 minutes each side), then supplement with formula if the baby is still hungry. Supplement volume is determined by the baby's behavior, not a formula.

When feedings are too few

Red flags ‚Äî see a doctor:

  • Fewer than 4 wet diapers in 24 hours (after day 4 of life)
  • Loss of more than 10% of birth weight by day 5
  • Hasn't returned to birth weight by day 14
  • Baby is lethargic, doesn't wake for feedings
  • Urine is dark and concentrated

What to record

For each feeding, record three things:

  1. Start time. After a week of records, you'll see your baby's natural rhythm.
  2. Type and volume. Breast (which side, how many minutes) or bottle (how many ml).
  3. Behavior after. Fell asleep, active, spit up. Helps distinguish overfeeding from normal.

When to start: WHO recommendations

WHO recommends exclusive breastfeeding until 6 months. Complementary feeding starts at 6 months. Not before 4 and not after 6 ‚Äî this is the "tolerance window" when the immune system best accepts new foods.

Age is a necessary but not sufficient condition. The child must be physiologically ready.

Signs of readiness

All four signs must be present simultaneously:

  1. Sits with support. Holds head confidently, doesn't slump.
  2. Tongue-thrust reflex is gone. Doesn't push food out of mouth with tongue.
  3. Shows interest in food. Reaches for your plate, opens mouth, follows the spoon.
  4. Doubled birth weight. Usually happens by 4‚Äì5 months.
"Not enough milk," "doesn't get full from breast," "wakes at night" ‚Äî these are NOT signs of readiness for solids.

How to measure: three methods

Accuracy depends on the method:

  • Rectal ‚Äî most accurate. Shows "true" body temperature. Normal ‚Äî up to 38.0 ¬∞C. Recommended for children under 3 months.
  • Axillary (armpit) ‚Äî most common. Reads 0.3‚Äì0.6 ¬∞C lower than rectal. Normal ‚Äî up to 37.5 ¬∞C. Measurement time ‚Äî 5 minutes.
  • Infrared (ear/forehead) ‚Äî fast (1‚Äì3 seconds), but margin of error ¬±0.2‚Äì0.4 ¬∞C. Good for screening; at high readings, verify with axillary.
Same thermometer, same spot, same time of day. That's the only way data is comparable.

When to reduce temperature

Temperature is not the illness ‚Äî it's an immune response. Reducing it "just because it's high" isn't necessary. Focus on how the child feels:

  • Below 38.5 ¬∞C ‚Äî don't reduce if the child is active, drinking, playing. The body is fighting infection.
  • 38.5‚Äì39.0 ¬∞C ‚Äî reduce if the child is lethargic, refuses drinks, cries, sleeps poorly.
  • Above 39.0 ¬∞C ‚Äî always reduce.
Exception: for babies under 3 months, reduce any temperature above 38.0 ¬∞C and contact a doctor immediately.

Action plan

Step-by-step guide for elevated temperature:

  1. Measure accurately. Axillary thermometer, 5 minutes, child at rest. Record the result in Lummo.
  2. Assess the child's state. Active and drinking ‚Äî observe. Lethargic and refusing fluids ‚Äî act.
  3. Give antipyretics. Ibuprofen (from 3 months) or paracetamol (from birth). Dose strictly by weight, not age.
  4. Plenty of fluids. Breast milk, water, compote. High temperature = fluid loss.
  5. Ventilate the room. Optimal 20‚Äì22 ¬∞C. Don't overdress the child ‚Äî heat dissipation through skin is important.
  6. Re-measure after 30‚Äì40 minutes. Temperature should start dropping. If not ‚Äî call a doctor.

When to call a doctor

Call emergency or your pediatrician immediately if:

  • Child is under 3 months and temperature is above 38.0 ¬∞C
  • Temperature is above 39.5 ¬∞C and doesn't decrease after antipyretics
  • Temperature persists for more than 3 days
  • Seizures (febrile) have occurred
  • There's a rash that doesn't blanch when pressed
  • Child hasn't urinated for 6+ hours
  • Child is unresponsive, crying inconsolably, refusing all fluids
Record each measurement with exact time. At the doctor's visit, this shows the trend: rising, holding steady, or falling.
